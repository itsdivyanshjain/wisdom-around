During my childhood, my elder sister used to ask me who I loved more -- her or my younger sister? I used to say “both.” Most probably, this answer came from the heart that I indeed love both of them or maybe it could be not wanting to hurt others. At that age, I don’t think I’ve developed cognitive abilities to reason.

The important thing is about the answer “both”. Definitely on a rational scale, I may have liked one more than the other. So, let me ask you the question: Whom do you love more - “Mom or Dad”?

If your answer is either your “mom or dad”, then the loser of that answer is... you. That doesn’t mean that your answer is invalid; but it’s essential to ask this validity of question itself, like how knowing who do you love more is going to be helpful?

Let’s say you’ve said “mom”. But does that make dad less loving towards you? Often, dads have hard times expressing themselves, which may seem to us as if they love us less.

But let me again ask you the question: If you had to live with either one of them, who would you choose?

## Limitation Of Binary

If you loved both equally, you might struggle with this answer. And for kids, it’s generally more healthier to have both parents compared to choosing between them.

We’ve often learned to make comparisons of our choices. Comparison is not just the thief of joy but also often results in a race to the bottom. You think there’s one right or wrong option, and you must be loving one more than others. And once you buy into this mentality, the more you try to prove one better over the other, the more you’ll lose yourself.

So what does this mean to us? we often face this conundrum on daily basis like choosing between:<br>
- Dream and Family
- Career and Relationship
- Physical Health and Mental Health
- Rationality and Irrationality
- Atheism and Religion (& more)

Beyond these daily dilemmas, we’re also constantly pushed to make choices, pick sides, have opinions, and get more polarized due to the amplification of social media.

In general choices aren't mutually exclusive they do serve different needs and purpose, **hence both the choices are valid, and it’s ok to want both of them in your life.** However, does this mean we can never make definite choices or commit to anything? 

## Where Our Commitment Lies?

**We do commit, but not to those choices; instead, we commit to our present moment.** When we commit to the present, there are no “ifs,” “buts,” “shoulds,” or “maybes.” we’ll learn to make the most out of the current scenario.

If you feel like choosing between your dream and family, you can choose your dream on working days and spend time with your family during festivals and important moments. 

By giving attention to those things that matter most at that particular moment, you’ll find a balance that works for you.

## What lies ahead?

We also need to accept that reality can often be messy, unclear, and uncertain. In such situations, the best way to make progress or find clarity is by taking action first and trusting that we can refine and improve it later.
